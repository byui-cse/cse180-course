Algorithmic Thinking

CSE 180

Syllabus

Course Description

In this course you will have the opportunity to gain the knowledge and practice required to design and apply algorithms to solve personal, spiritual, and personal challenges you have in your life, and help others apply the same to their lives. This course builds your ability to see yourself as a logical, mathematical thinker, with power and ability to make good, defensible decisions and communicate your thinking to others, along with why the decisions you have made are good.

Design Principles

This course is based on one underlying principle; namely, that you are a dedicated disciple of Jesus Christ and therefore you would never lie or make deceptive claims regarding your understanding of the course material. Nor would you lie or deceive regarding completion of the tasks you will be assigned. Other principles include:

  • lifting others in the class;
  • using your agency;
  • showing empathy and love; and
  • being humble enough to ask for help from your teacher and others in the class.

All tasks assigned to you in this course are to be completed in teams. Your instructor will inform you regarding team creation. It is also true that you will never be given a team grade. All of us here on earth work together to aid each other in achieving salvation and exaltation, yet we are individually responsible for our own salvation and exaltation. Nobody makes it to the Celestial Kingdom because they belonged to a group of good people. Neither should you expect to get an A just because you are part of a group of hardworking students that come to understand the topics of this course and are able to apply them.

Therefore, when you are helping someone you should never give them your own work. Neither should you expect someone to give you theirs when you ask them for help. Instead, help those who will come to you by talking over how they are thinking about and approaching their tasks, showing them how to search for and find answers, and be loving and supportive. In other words, teach them just like you would teach someone the Gospel of Jesus Christ. Love them, help them find their own answers, and lead them to the scriptures.

Course Outcomes

When you successfully complete this course, you will be able to:

  1. solve life-challenges where algorithmic thinking skills are needed to design and create an algorithm that can then be used to inform decisions or draw conclusions,
  2. use algorithmic thinking to assess what key variables are needed and to illuminate key assumptions made when creating and applying an algorithm to life-challenges,
  3. use self-designed algorithms to make informed decisions,
  4. interpret quantitative data regarding the quality of self-designed algorithms, and
  5. communicate self-designed algorithms to others in an efficient, mathematical, way.

Course Structure

This is a semester-long course. In weeks 1 through 6 you will have assigned readings and quizzes, and an interaction with other class members as you work to understand principles of algorithms and their application. You will discover and apply these algorithms in your life. In week 7 you will use what you have learned to help others by finding algorithms they use in their lives that they may not be aware of. In weeks 8 through 12 you will explore how to evaluate the efficiency of the algorithms you discovered in weeks 1 through 6 to help you improve your use of them in your life. In week 13 you will explore the efficiency of the algorithms you found in week 7 to help others improve their lives. In week 14 you will report on the growth you have achieved over the semester due to your increased understanding of algorithms.

Assessments

  • Week 1

    You will introduce yourself to the class and ponder how you can change your attitude towards mathematics.

  • Week 2 through Week 6

    You will take a quiz after completing the assigned reading, and you will explore different types of algorithms.

  • Week 7

    You will, as a project, discover and appropriately describe algorithms others use in their lives.

  • Week 8 through Week 12

    You will take a quiz after completing the assigned reading, and you will explore how to use calculation to classify and assess algorithms by their efficiency.

  • Week 13

    You will, as a project, correctly evaluate for efficiency algorithms you have discovered and described that others use in their daily educational, spiritual, and personal lives, and suggest improved alternatives.

  • Week 14

    You will ponder your experience in the class and write a self-assessment report, including the final grade you will claim for the course.

From the University Catalog

As you assess your learning for your grade calculation, apply these definitions from the catalog.

  • “A” represents outstanding understanding, application, and integration of subject material and extensive evidence of original thinking, skillful use of concepts, and ability to analyze and solve complex problems. Demonstrates diligent application of Learning Model principles, including initiative in serving other students.

  • “B” represents considerable/significant understanding, application, and incorporation of the material which would prepare a student to be successful in next level courses, graduate school or employment. The student participates in the Learning Model as applied in the course.

  • “C” represents sufficient understanding of subject matter. The student demonstrates minimal initiative to be prepared for class. Sequenced courses could be attempted but mastering new materials might prove challenging. The student participates only marginally in the Learning Model.

  • “D” represents poor performance and initiative to learn and understand and apply course materials. Retaking a course or remediation may be necessary to prepare for additional instruction in this subject matter.

  • “F” represents failure in the course.
