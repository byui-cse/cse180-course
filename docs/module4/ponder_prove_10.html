CSE 180: Week 10

Ponder and Prove

Your Tasks

This week you will begin to explore how to use calculation to assess and classify way-point and choosing algorithms by their efficiency. These are often called shortest path algorithms.

When you have a task to perform, what is the shortest path to completion of that task?

For a hypothetical (certainly not real!) example: In high school, suppose you were bad — maybe you split an infinitive, or used the passive voice — and the teacher made you go up to the board and write “The passive voice will not be used by me.” 100 times. A silly example, perhaps, but what is a fast way to do that? If you can use a computer and a word processing program, then think about the slow way — typing that sentence a hundred times — versus the fast way: typing it once, copying it, then pasting it 99 more times.

Of course, the fastest way would be to somehow tell your word processor program how many times to paste and then have it do all the pasting for you!

Show someone the USA graph and ask them to think about how they would find the shortest path (driving-distance-wise) from Sacramento California to Augusta Maine.

Observe their process, figure out what algorithm they were using. Figure out the complexity of their algorithm and compare it to that of your own.

Also, calculate the O-complexity of the algorithms you collected during week 4. Could any of these algorithms be improved?

How to Fulfill Return-and-Report Requirements

  • Make sure you take notes and write a report for your teammates on the conversations you have outside of class. Here are some ideas you can use to improve the quality of the report.

    • What are your relationships to the people you interviewed?
    • What are the detailed, discrete, algorithm steps they used?
    • Why did they choose to do it that way?
    • What are your ideas for how they could improve this algorithm?
    Do not limit yourself to these questions.

  • Prepare to share with your classroom team your report and discoveries about the algorithms you investigated.
