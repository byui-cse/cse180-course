Algorithmic Thinking

CSE 180

Syllabus

Course Description

In this course you will have the opportunity to gain the knowledge and practice required to design and apply algorithms to solve personal and spiritual challenges you have in your life, and help others apply the same to their lives. This course builds your ability to see yourself as a logical, algorithmic thinker, with power and ability to make good, defensible decisions and communicate your thinking to others, along with why the decisions you have made are good.

Design Principles

This course is based on one underlying principle; namely, that you are a dedicated disciple of Jesus Christ and therefore you would never lie or make deceptive claims regarding your understanding of the course material. Nor would you lie or deceive regarding completion of the tasks you will be assigned. Other principles include:

  • lifting others in the class;
  • using your agency;
  • showing empathy and love; and
  • being humble enough to ask for help from your teacher and others in the class.

Many tasks assigned to you in this course are to be completed in teams. Your instructor will inform you regarding team creation. It is also true that you will never be given a team grade. You will be accountable to your team, and will report to them during class meetings (but never outside of class) the results of your investigations and collaborations outside of class. But no tasks done outside of class will be instructor- or peer-reviewed.

Teamwork is part of the BYU-Idaho Learning Model, and indeed, it is part of the Plan of Salvation. All of us here on earth work together to aid each other in achieving salvation and exaltation, yet we are individually responsible for our own salvation and exaltation. Nobody makes it to the Celestial Kingdom because they belonged to a group of good people. Neither should you expect to get an A just because you are part of a group of hardworking students that come to understand the topics of this course and are able to apply them.

Therefore, when you are helping someone you should never give them your own work. Neither should you expect someone to give you theirs when you ask them for help. Instead, help those who will come to you by talking over how they are thinking about and approaching their tasks, showing them how to search for and find answers, and be loving and supportive. In other words, teach them just like you would teach someone the Gospel of Jesus Christ. Love them, help them find their own answers, and lead them to the scriptures.

None.

Platforms, Requirements, and Resources

You will need a laptop computer running MacOS, Windows, or Linux. Internet connectivity is also required.

There is no text, all readings are found in iLearn or the BYU-Idaho Library.

Course Outcomes

When you successfully complete this course, you will be able to:

  1. Create algorithms to achieve specific goals
  2. Recognize existing algorithms in everyday life
  3. Identify the characteristics of algorithms
  4. Decompose algorithms into discrete steps
  5. Improve algorithms

Course Structure

The first week will be an introduction (Module 1), followed by 2-week modules that will help organize our exploration of algorithms in our lives and as they apply to problems that we will solve in our careers. Halfway through, you'll have the opportunity to do a "Grade Claim" where you meet 1-1 with the instructor to claim the grade that best represents your progress. The last module will be documenting and showing that you can apply the principles we've learned throughout the semester and have a "Final Grade Claim" with the instructor. The initial grade claim is worth 10% and the final grade claim is the other 90% of the grade.

Assessments

  • Week 1 (Module 1)

    We'll explore the concept of an algorithm and figure out where we see them in our life. We'll also look at how to tell if one algorithm is better than another using Big O notation.

  • Weeks 2 & 3 (Module 2) - Algorithms for Ordering
  • Weeks 4 & 5 (Module 3) - Algorithms for Ordered Searching
  • Weeks 6 & 7 (Module 4) - Algorithms for Making Choices

    You'll have an initial grade claim that is 10% of your final grade.

  • Weeks 8 & 9 (Module 5) - Greedy Algorithms
  • Weeks 10 & 11 (Module 6) - Algorithms for Eliminating Waste
  • Weeks 12 & 14 (Module 7) - Algorithms You Use

    You will ponder your experience in the class and write a self-assessment report, including the final grade you will claim for the course, taking into account your peers’ evaluations of your work. You will also consider attendance as it is required throughout the semester. This will account for 90% of your final grade in the course.

From the University Catalog

As you assess your learning for your final grade claim, apply these definitions from the catalog.
